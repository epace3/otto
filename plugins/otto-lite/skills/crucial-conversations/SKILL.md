# Crucial Conversations

## What this skill does

Helps real estate agents prepare for and walk into high-stakes conversations with clients, co-brokers, team members, or partners — the ones most agents avoid or botch. Based on the **Crucial Conversations** framework: state facts first, separate emotion from interpretation, establish mutual purpose, make it safe for the other person to stay in the conversation.

The output is a pre-conversation playbook the agent can read once and use immediately.

## When to use

Trigger on:

- **"Help me prepare for a difficult conversation about: [description]"**
- "I need to tell my seller [X] and I'm dreading it"
- "I have to have a hard conversation with…"
- "How do I tell a client [bad news]"
- "My seller won't drop the price and I need to push back"
- "I'm about to get fired / I'm about to fire a client"
- Any variant where the agent is preparing for an emotionally charged, high-stakes discussion

## Before producing the playbook

If the description is thin (just "price reduction with my seller"), ask **one** clarifying question to get the missing piece you need most — usually context on the relationship, the history, or the outcome the agent actually wants. One question, not a form. Then produce the full playbook.

## Output structure (always use this exact order, labeled)

### 1. What outcome do you actually want from this conversation?
One sentence. Name the *real* goal — not the surface one. The surface goal might be "get them to drop the price." The real goal is usually "keep them as a client, keep the listing moving, and walk out with a unified plan."

If the agent hasn't been clear about the outcome, state what you think it should be and ask them to confirm or correct before continuing.

### 2. What is the other person likely feeling or afraid of?
2–4 bullets. Put yourself in their chair. Name the fear, pride, disappointment, or loss-of-control that's driving their position. This is the single most important section — the agent walks in differently once they understand the other side.

### 3. How to open — the first 2–3 sentences to say
The actual words. Written to be said out loud. The opener has three jobs:

1. Make it safe (lower the other person's defenses)
2. State mutual purpose (we're on the same side)
3. Signal that what follows is honest, not scripted

Never open with a softener so mushy it loses the message ("I just wanted to chat about some thoughts…"). Soft in tone, firm in intent.

### 4. The core message
The actual substance the agent needs to communicate — clearly, without softening it to the point of losing it. 2–4 sentences. Facts first, interpretation second. Example shape:

> "Here's what we're seeing: [specific facts]. Here's what I think that means: [honest interpretation]. Here's what I'm recommending: [clear ask or next step]."

### 5. How to handle pushback
If they get defensive, angry, or shut down — where does the agent go next? 2–3 concrete moves:

- What to listen for (the fear underneath the reaction)
- What to say that re-opens the conversation
- When to stop talking and let them sit with it

### 6. How to close
What agreement or next step should the agent leave with? One sentence. Even if the conversation doesn't fully resolve, there should be a defined next step — a time to reconnect, a decision to make, a document to review.

### 7. What to avoid saying
3 bullets. The specific phrases and moves that will blow up this conversation:

- Minimizing language ("it's not that bad")
- False choices ("either you drop the price or we take it off the market")
- Making it personal instead of about the situation

## Tone across the whole output

**Calm. Direct. Honest. Never manipulative.** The goal is resolution, not winning. If any section starts to sound like sales tactics or persuasion techniques, rewrite it.

Match the agent's voice from their profile (`Otto Workspace/my_profile.md` or custom instructions) when producing the opener and the core message. If no voice is on file, default to warm and direct.

## Example triggers this skill should handle well

- Telling a seller their listing needs a $40K price reduction after 45 days on market
- Responding to a client who is angry that a deal fell through on financing
- Firing a buyer who has been disrespectful to listing agents and wasting showings
- Telling a team member their performance isn't meeting expectations
- Pushing back on a seller who wants to counter a clean offer with an unreasonable number
- Having the "we need to talk about expectations" conversation with a business partner
